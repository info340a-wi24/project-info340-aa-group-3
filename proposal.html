Protest Hub Proposal

Problem Description

Over time, people have turned to revolts, movements, and protests as a means of advocating for social change. With the rise of social media in the modern era, diverse groups of people are increasingly connected with issues across the globe, as half a million people attended Black Lives Matter protests on June 6th, 2020 (Buchanan et al., 2020). In response to these shifts in the availability of information, apps such as Instagram have worked to expand journalism efforts through features for fundraising, geotags and hashtags, and story posts to rapidly spread information. With the virality of social media, large masses of people can successfully gather and be reached through activist accounts with calls to action.

While this is helpful, no centralized platform exists to personalize the process of protest involvement. For issues that are smaller in scale, hosted by lesser-known organizations, or in less metropolitan areas, coverage becomes increasingly difficult. Particularly for individuals whose personalized content and social circles fail to include current events, it is difficult to consistently organize in cohesive ways or discover events that perfectly align with one’s preferences. Furthermore, in response to the popularity of protests over social media, many governmental officials are banning social media or utilizing the platforms to infringe on the privacy of citizens (Cortés, 2021). To better empower the public to speak up in meaningful ways, a new solution is required.

Application Description

To address the following problem space, we propose the following features for a new Protest Hub application:

  • Protest Search Function
  • Protest RSVP Forms
  • Protest Map Exploration Tool

When users navigate to our site, they will have a chance to utilize a primary search function, which includes various filters and tags to narrow down to one’s preferred set of protest recommendations. Similar to discovery apps such as Yelp, users can enter keywords on preferences such as location, cause, and organization. Additionally, subcategories for filtering will be provided via checkboxes and listed options, such as date and time ranges, or number of attendees. Based on the user preferences provided, the application will provide a list of matching protests for users to learn more about and RSVP for. For this feature, the click of a button will present users with a form to collect information on their name and email, effectively adding them to the list of attendees. After filling out the form, users will be notified via email about their successful RSVP. Lastly, for users who are interested in broader exploration, a map exploration tool can be accessed to discover a variety of protests across the U.S. By zooming in and out within the map, as well as clicking on relevant protest location pins, users can quickly discover events close by and become exposed to a greater variety of protest topics in a shortened form.

Given this difficulty of accessing current data, we may have to manually collect data, create dummy datasets, or mimic functionality through historic protest information. To handle user input, some functionality must be introduced that allows for the tracking of providing emails in a spreadsheet that can then correspond to notification messages that are sent out through our application.

This Protest Hub is meant to be used by anyone who wants to bring attention to any existing issues and those who want to discover protests happening in their area. The included past protests will also give people insight into the issues that are going on. With this, we hope that minority groups canhave a stronger voice and that protests can reach more people who are in the area. Users will also have an easier time organizing and preparing for the protests that they plan to participate in. In doing so, people who work long hours will be able to attend and contribute to the protests that mean a lot to them. By creating this centralized platform that has all the information about both current and past protests, people who care about the same issues will have a place to find others with the same views as themselves. It will also be useful to gauge the size of the protest using the RSVP information. Preemptively knowing the estimated size of the protest can help organizers determine whether they need more advertising. Other users may also feel more inclined to participate in a protest that is likely to have more attendance as numbers will ensure a greater impact made because of the event.
